Case Study

K. pneumoniae ST17 Co-producing KPC-2 and NDM-1

Genomic analysis reveals a maximum-risk pathogen with resistance to nearly all available treatment options.

By Felipe Alberto Lei • October 12, 2025

The Critical Threat

Klebsiella pneumoniae is one of the leading causes of hospital-acquired infections worldwide, and the emergence of carbapenemase-producing strains represents an unprecedented challenge for modern medicine. The isolate HGPE-001-KPN, belonging to the rare ST17 clone, goes even further: it co-produces two of the most feared carbapenemases - KPC-2 and NDM-1 - creating a therapeutic "hole" that compromises virtually all available treatment options.

Profile of the Hyper-Resistant Pathogen

Complete genomic analysis revealed alarming characteristics of this isolate:

  • Sequence Type ST17: A rare clone, but of extremely high risk due to its extreme resistance profile.
  • Dual carbapenemase production: Simultaneous presence of blaKPC-2 and blaNDM-1 genes, conferring resistance to different classes of β-lactams.
  • Unmatched genetic arsenal: 18 resistance genes in total, the highest number observed in the entire studied cohort, conferring resistance to multiple antibiotic classes.
  • Mobile genetic elements: Presence of plasmids and transposons that facilitate horizontal transfer of resistance genes to other bacteria.

The Therapeutic Impasse

The co-production of KPC-2 and NDM-1 creates a particularly dangerous situation:

  • KPC-2 confers resistance to carbapenems but can be partially inhibited by new β-lactamase inhibitors such as avibactam.
  • NDM-1 is a metallo-β-lactamase that is not inhibited by traditional β-lactamase inhibitors and confers even broader resistance.
  • The combination neutralizes the main treatment strategies, including combinations of last-resort antibiotics.

Public Health Implications

This case exemplifies the worst-case scenario of the post-antibiotic era: a common pathogen that has become virtually untreatable. The identification and characterization of these isolates through genomics is fundamental to:

  • Implement rigorous infection control measures to prevent dissemination.
  • Guide therapeutic decisions based on the molecular resistance profile.
  • Develop new antimicrobial strategies and therapeutic targets.
  • Monitor the emergence and dissemination of high-risk clones.

Conclusion

The genomic characterization of isolates like HGPE-001-KPN demonstrates the critical importance of bioinformatics and molecular surveillance in the era of antimicrobial resistance. Only through a deep understanding of the genetic mechanisms involved can we develop effective strategies to combat these emerging threats to global health.
